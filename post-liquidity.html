The liquidity model nobody wants to own

March 2026 · 3 min read

Every finance team has a liquidity model somewhere, usually inherited from someone who left two years ago. Nobody wants to own it, because owning it means being the person who gets the call on a Friday afternoon asking whether payroll clears on Monday.

Why it gets neglected

Cash forecasting doesn't produce a headline number for the board deck the way revenue or margin does. It's plumbing — invisible when it works, catastrophic when it doesn't. That asymmetry means it's chronically under-invested in relative to the risk it carries.

A forecasting model earns its keep exactly once a year. The other eleven months, it just has to not be wrong.

The version I inherited at Northbridge was a single spreadsheet with thirteen hidden tabs and one formula error that had been silently overstating available cash by roughly 4% for two quarters. Rebuilding it wasn't glamorous work, but it's the reason month-end close got three days shorter and the CFO stopped asking treasury for a manual sanity check every cycle.

What a good version actually needs

Rolling thirteen-week granularity, a clear separation between committed and discretionary outflows, and FX exposure broken out by currency rather than netted into a single number. None of it is sophisticated. It just has to be trusted enough that people stop double-checking it by hand.
