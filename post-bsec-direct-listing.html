BSEC's direct listing reform is a real fix for a real bottleneck

July 2026 · 3 min read

The news: The Bangladesh Securities and Exchange Commission has announced plans to let large, well-governed private companies list directly on the stock exchange after floating just 10% of their shares — down from the 25% minimum currently required, and previously available only to state-owned enterprises. BSEC Chairman Mr. Masud Khan named companies like Unilever Bangladesh and Incepta Pharmaceuticals as the kind of firms this could bring to market, and said the rule change is expected within three months. Alongside it, the BSEC is delegating more real-time surveillance authority to the Dhaka Stock Exchange, moving to shorten the settlement cycle from T+2 to T+1, and working on stricter enforcement against market manipulators.

Why this matters

In Bangladesh, the lengthy and complex IPO listing process has become a major bottleneck, discouraging high-quality companies — particularly multinational corporations — from accessing the capital market. If the BSEC follows through on amending the existing regulations to allow direct listing, it could attract fundamentally strong companies to the exchange, broaden the market, and enhance investor confidence. The presence of more quality companies would also make the Bangladesh capital market more attractive to foreign investors.

A shorter path to market only helps if what arrives at the end of it can still be trusted.

The condition that matters most

However, while introducing direct listing, the regulator must ensure that the interests of both retail and institutional investors are adequately protected. Robust due diligence, transparent valuation and price discovery mechanisms, strong disclosure requirements, and effective market surveillance should be in place to prevent market manipulation and ensure fair pricing.

With these safeguards, direct listing could become a significant step toward developing a deeper, more efficient, and more credible capital market in Bangladesh.
