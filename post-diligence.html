What due diligence actually catches (and what it doesn't)

June 2026 · 4 min read

Most deals don't die on the numbers. They die on the things nobody thought to put in a data room in the first place — an informal side agreement with a key customer, a founder who's the only person who understands the pricing logic, a piece of IP that was never actually assigned to the company.

The model finds what you ask it to find

A diligence checklist is a hypothesis about what could go wrong. It's built from precedent — the last twenty deals, the standard risk categories, the things counsel always flags. That's useful, but it also means it's structurally blind to anything genuinely novel about the target.

The best diligence finding I ever made wasn't in a spreadsheet. It was in a Slack export nobody expected us to read.

Financial diligence is good at what it's designed for: verifying that the numbers in the CIM are the numbers in the ledger, that revenue recognition is defensible, that working capital assumptions aren't quietly propping up the growth story. It's much weaker at catching organizational risk — key-person dependency, culture mismatch, the informal processes that don't show up until integration.

What actually helps

Spend time with the people two levels below the people you're supposed to talk to. Ask what would break if a specific person left tomorrow. Read the customer contracts yourself instead of trusting the summary. None of this replaces the model — it's what tells you whether the model's assumptions are worth trusting in the first place.
